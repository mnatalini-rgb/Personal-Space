Data Analysis — The 8-Step Investigation

Cross-platform analysis: CDP × BQ

We cross-referenced Snowflake CDP data (152,939 Winline depositors) against BQ user profiles, mission state, campaign configs, and shop data.

Steps 1–3: Finding the cohort

1. CDP Overlap: Only 9% of 152K Winline depositors appear in CDP mission events. 139,120 are "deposit-only."
2. FACEIT Activity: Of 139K deposit-only, 45,664 (33%) are active gamers (match in 30d). Near-identical profile to mission-engaged users.
3. BOFU Activation: 43,904 (96.2%) of active deposit-only users are already in FTD/WHALE BOFU missions. They play matches, claim gaming rewards. Only 65 (0.1%) complete partner tasks.

Conscious engagement validated: 43,905 users have status = 'complete' on the gaming task — they claimed the reward. They saw the mission, collected FP, looked at "Deposit 3,000 RUB", and chose not to proceed.

Steps 4–8: Understanding why

4. Staleness: FTD & WHALE partner challenges are identical Feb = Mar = Apr 2026. Same copy, same amounts, same odds.
5. FTD vs WHALE: WHALE converts 7pp lower (38.4% vs 45.6%). Higher threshold = lower conversion. No intermediate steps.
6. Cross-Mission: 30.2% are mission grinders (also do Premium Monthly). 69.8% are BOFU-only. Two different intervention paths.
7. Historical: 9.5% → 6.7% over 18 months. Actively worsening.
8. Shop: 83.3% spend FP in shop. Profile items dominate (831M FP). 7.7K buy Tradeit codes voluntarily with FP — but won't do Winline partner tasks.

Key Insight

43,904 users are in our missions.
They choose not to convert.

Four structural problems

1. Partner task conversion failure — 43,904 active BOFU users, 65 complete partner tasks (0.1%). The gaming→partner gap applies to BOFU just as much as TOFU.
2. Challenge staleness — identical partner asks for 3 consecutive months. Zero incremental motivation.
3. Declining trend — 9.5% → 6.7% over 18 months while user base tripled. Getting worse, not plateauing.
4. Reward mismatch — 83.3% actively spend FP in shop. They are economically motivated. But current partner task rewards don't justify depositing 3,000–5,000 RUB.

What this means

The original hypothesis was wrong.

We assumed "nonlinear structure" (letting users choose challenge order) would solve the conversion problem.

The data shows: BOFU users already see all challenges. They're not blocked by order — they're choosing not to act on stale, poorly-incentivised partner tasks.

Nonlinear structure helps TOFU. For BOFU, we need challenge variety and reward rebalancing. Hence: three pillars.

Pillar 1 — Detail

Eng Design Nonlinear Mission Structure

What changes

  • Challenge state model refactor: Current backend assumes sequential ordering (N unlocks after N-1). Needs concurrent active challenges with independent completion states.
  • New config schema: mode: sequential | nonlinear | hybrid per mission. Hybrid allows some challenges sequential, others flexible.
  • Progression engine: New component to calculate % complete, emit milestone events. Support configurable weighting (partner challenges can count more).
  • Auto-claim: Existing reward-on-completion must work for any challenge independently.
  • Instrumentation: New BQ events for challenge selection order, progression milestones, time-to-completion per challenge type.

Design UX requirements

  • Mission map/board: All challenges visible on entry. Visual distinction between gaming vs partner challenges.
  • Progress tracker: Bar, tier map, or board — must show overall advancement and distance to next milestone reward.
  • Soft recommendations: "Start here for best results" guidance without hard blocking.
  • Trust Modal: Must remain in AL flow (validated: nearly doubles AL step completion).

Key question for Engineering: What's the effort estimate for the challenge state refactor? This is the critical path item for Pillar 1. Everything else (progression engine, config, UI) layers on top.

Pillar 2 — Detail

Eng Ops Comm Progressive Challenges & Rotation

Challenge template library

  • New data model: Partner-approved challenge variants per tier. Each template: action type, threshold, currency, min odds, localised copy, reward tier.
  • Versioned + validated: Templates enforce contractual constraints (min deposit amounts, odds thresholds). Activation Ops can't accidentally go below partner minimums.
  • Progressive graph: Partner challenges form ordered sub-sequences within nonlinear mission (500 → 1,500 → 3,000 RUB).

Monthly rotation

  • MVP: Manual template assignment by Ops per month (no automation). Low-risk, immediate benefit.
  • Phase 2: Automated scheduler — ensures no user sees same partner ask two consecutive months. Tracks last_assigned_template_id per user-tier.

Why this matters (the data)

Pillar 3 — Detail

Eng Design Ops Reward Rebalancing

What we're building

  • Milestone reward system: Thresholds like "complete ≥3 challenges incl. ≥1 partner" to unlock bonus rewards. Configurable per mission.
  • Exclusive items: Profile cards, avatar frames, limited-edition bundles — flagged mission_exclusive, cannot be bought in shop. Scarcity drives value.
  • Tiered reward curve: Low-tier partner task = FP only. Mid-tier = FP + common item. High-tier = exclusive item + Mystery Box.
  • Mystery Box on milestones: Reuse existing mechanic (validated +61% AL uplift, Jan/Feb 2026). Adapt to trigger on milestone events, not just AL.

Eng Technical scope

  • Milestone layer on top of per-challenge rewards
  • Shop item catalogue integration (mission_exclusive flag)
  • Reward analytics: BQ events for reward type effectiveness

Why this works (the data)

83.3% of conscious BOFU users are active FP shop spenders. Here's what they buy:

Profile customization dominates. These users care about FACEIT identity/status. Exclusive items they can't get from gaming alone = strong incentive to complete partner tasks.

Design Action needed

Design mission-exclusive profile cards, frames, and bundles. Start with recolours of popular items or limited-edition variants to minimise asset creation lead time.
